Your test says the pricing change increased conversions by 12%. But here's what nobody in the room is asking: what happens when your competitors respond?

## The problem with partial equilibrium thinking

Most A/B tests operate in what economists call *partial equilibrium* — they measure the effect of a change while holding everything else constant. That's fine for button colors. It's dangerous for pricing.

When you cut prices by 10% and measure a conversion lift, you're seeing a *short-run* effect. The experiment doesn't capture:

- **Competitor responses**: If your price cut works, competitors will notice. And respond.
- **Demand-side adaptation**: Customers learn. Early adopters behave differently from the steady state.
- **Supply-side constraints**: Can you actually fulfill 12% more orders at the same margin?

## The question isn't whether your experiment worked

It's whether the result will survive contact with the market.

67% of pricing tests miss equilibrium effects entirely. Teams ship the "winning" variant, celebrate the lift, and then watch the numbers quietly regress over the next quarter.

The long-run impact is often overestimated by **3x** or more.

## What to do instead

1. **Ask the equilibrium question before you ship**: "If this works, who responds, and how does that change the outcome?"
2. **Run longer hold-out windows**: Don't measure pricing changes over 2 weeks. Give the market time to adjust.
3. **Model the competitive response**: Even a rough game-theoretic sketch is better than assuming competitors are statues.
4. **Separate novelty from steady state**: Your first-week lift is not your long-run lift.

The economics lens doesn't replace experimentation — it tells you which experiments to trust.
